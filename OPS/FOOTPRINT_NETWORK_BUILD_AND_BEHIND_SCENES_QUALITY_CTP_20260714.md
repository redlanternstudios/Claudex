## THREE PASS ANALYSIS

### PASS 1: SURFACE

Footprint should invite good builders and hiring professionals, feature credible work, share opportunities, and make relevant introductions.

### PASS 2: CHALLENGE

Inviting good people is not a network method. Good people can still receive weak service, irrelevant content, careless introductions, or no response.

A large candidate pool is not valuable if talent partners must perform the same filtering they perform elsewhere.

A large opportunity pool is not valuable if builders see vague, stale, exploitative, or irrelevant demand.

Automation does not solve weak judgment. It increases the volume and speed of whatever judgment already exists.

### PASS 3: GOVERNING TRUTH

Footprint must build the network through service, not acquisition.

The atomic unit is not a member, post, profile, or connection. The atomic unit is one useful route produced from a verified need, credible evidence, clear consent, and accountable judgment.

Public trust should emerge from the repeated quality of private work. Growth should occur only when the current operating team can serve more people without lowering judgment quality or response reliability.

Pass 3 governs this CTP.

## MAJOR POINT A: NETWORK FORMATION THROUGH SERVICE

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: Invite builders and talent partners into one curated founding cohort.
2. Root cause: Early networks fail when they collect people before proving a repeated exchange of value.
3. First order consequence: Invitation follows a specific reason Footprint can help or learn from the person.
4. Second order consequence: Participants experience usefulness before they are asked to contribute attention, proof, or referrals.
5. Third order consequence: Strong private experiences produce voluntary referrals and a more trusted next cohort.
6. Upstream dependencies: Steward capacity, participant criteria, role and intent capture, evidence standards, demand standards, consent, and a useful route catalog.
7. Downstream dependencies: Intake, review, response, proof improvement, opportunity clarification, introductions, public assets, referrals, and outcomes.
8. Failure modes: Popularity based selection, founder friends receiving preference, builders without proof, talent partners without current demand, and invitations sent faster than service capacity.
9. Recovery paths: Pause invitations, explain the queue honestly, correct selection criteria, remove ineligible records, and review fairness evidence.
10. Strategic implication: Footprint grows through demonstrated care and useful outcomes rather than audience capture.

### FOUNDING COHORT METHOD

The founding cohort is invitation based and service led.

Every invitation records:

1. Why this person is relevant.
2. Which network side they represent.
3. What useful route Footprint can currently offer.
4. What Footprint hopes to learn.
5. Which steward owns the response.
6. Whether capacity exists before the invitation is sent.

No invitation is sent merely to increase follower or member count.

### NETWORK BUILD LOOP

1. Discover a person through trusted relationships, public work, referrals, or relevant participation.
2. Record the specific reason they may belong.
3. Invite them through a personal and permitted channel.
4. Clarify role, intent, evidence, demand, constraints, and consent.
5. Deliver one useful private route before requesting amplification or referral.
6. Publish only when public value, evidence, authority, and consent are all present.
7. Make an introduction only when both sides confirm relevance.
8. Follow the result without invasive tracking.
9. Record an Outcome Receipt.
10. Invite a referral only after useful service has been demonstrated.

## MAJOR POINT B: BEHIND THE SCENES QUALITY SYSTEM

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: A private team reviews people, proof, opportunities, content, and introductions.
2. Root cause: Public trust depends on decisions the audience does not see.
3. First order consequence: Each case needs an owner, evidence, a decision state, and a next action.
4. Second order consequence: Consistent review makes outcomes explainable and corrections possible.
5. Third order consequence: The accumulated decision history becomes a quality and matching asset.
6. Upstream dependencies: Role definitions, rubrics, training, case records, authority, private access boundaries, and independent review.
7. Downstream dependencies: Participant trust, content credibility, match quality, fairness, correction speed, and network reputation.
8. Failure modes: Inconsistent judgment, undocumented exceptions, superficial proof review, fake demand, slow responses, privacy leakage, and one reviewer controlling the full chain.
9. Recovery paths: Quarantine the case, assign a second review, correct the record, notify affected people when appropriate, retract public material, and update the rubric.
10. Strategic implication: Footprint quality is an operating discipline, not a brand voice or content style.

### PRIVATE SERVICE LOOP

Every participant case follows one canonical loop.

1. Intake.
2. Identity and role clarification.
3. Intent classification.
4. Evidence or demand review.
5. Useful route decision.
6. Participant response.
7. Optional proof improvement or opportunity clarification.
8. Optional public projection.
9. Optional mutual introduction.
10. Outcome observation.
11. Correction or closure.
12. Learning receipt.

Every state has an owner, timestamp, reason, evidence reference, next action, and expiry.

### QUALITY GATES

#### GATE 0: IDENTITY AND CONSENT

Required:

1. Canonical person and organization identity.
2. Current role.
3. Sharing permission.
4. Contact permission.
5. Correction and withdrawal path.

Failure result: Clarify, quarantine, or close. Never infer consent.

#### GATE 1: BUILDER PROOF

Required:

1. Clear problem.
2. Builder contribution.
3. Observable artifact or result.
4. Evidence source.
5. Claim ceiling.
6. Protected details.

Evidence classification:

1. Directly verified.
2. Corroborated.
3. Self reported.
4. Unsupported.

Failure result: Offer proof improvement. Do not inflate the claim or publicly feature the work.

#### GATE 2: TALENT DEMAND

Required:

1. Real organization or represented client.
2. Authority to share the demand.
3. Outcome the work must produce.
4. Required capabilities.
5. Location, timing, engagement, availability, and compensation constraints when applicable.
6. Current status and expiry.

Failure result: Clarify, correct, withdraw, or close. Do not route vague demand to builders.

#### GATE 3: USEFUL ROUTE

Required:

1. The route answers the participant's current intent.
2. The route is specific enough to act on.
3. The route does not create false hope.
4. The route has an accountable next step.

Valid routes include clarification, proof coaching, demand clarification, a resource, a private capability brief, a selective public asset, a mutual introduction, or safe silence with an honest reason.

#### GATE 4: PUBLIC PROJECTION

Required:

1. Reader value.
2. Evidence and claim ceiling.
3. Asset specific consent.
4. Identity authority.
5. Privacy review.
6. Historical exposure review.
7. Saturation approval.
8. Independent approval.

Failure result: Keep the value private or remain silent.

#### GATE 5: INTRODUCTION

Required:

1. Capability fit.
2. Evidence fit.
3. Demand fit.
4. Constraint fit.
5. Timing fit.
6. Mutual interest.
7. Consent from both sides.
8. An introduction brief stating why the connection may be useful without promising an outcome.

Failure result: Do not introduce. Explain the missing fit privately when useful.

#### GATE 6: OUTCOME AND LEARNING

Required:

1. Whether the route was useful.
2. Whether the introduction occurred.
3. Whether a conversation, interview, project, role, referral, collaboration, or other named outcome followed.
4. What helped.
5. What created friction.
6. Whether any correction or policy change is needed.

Failure result: Close honestly as no outcome, unknown outcome, or harmful outcome. Do not convert silence into success.

### OPERATING ROLES

The founding team needs five logical roles even if a small number of authorized people perform them.

1. Founding Steward owns relationships, invitations, participant communication, and final human care.
2. Evidence Steward reviews builder proof and claim ceilings.
3. Demand Steward reviews organizations, opportunities, constraints, and expiry.
4. Match Steward prepares relevance decisions and introduction briefs.
5. Trust Steward reviews consent, privacy, fairness, corrections, and incidents.

One person may perform more than one preparation role during the founding cohort. No person may create and independently approve the same public claim or introduction.

## MAJOR POINT C: AUTOMATION BOUNDARY

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: Use Supabase, n8n, SwarmClaw, analytics, and platform adapters to operate Footprint.
2. Root cause: Reliable service requires durable records, policy enforcement, reminders, routing, and auditability.
3. First order consequence: Automation reduces dropped cases and inconsistent procedural steps.
4. Second order consequence: Structured decision evidence reveals which judgments can later be automated safely.
5. Third order consequence: Stable policy automation increases capacity without hiding responsibility.
6. Upstream dependencies: Proven human process, approved data model, frozen rubrics, authority, access control, test fixtures, and recovery paths.
7. Downstream dependencies: Intake routing, case state, reminders, review queues, exposure limits, analytics, receipts, and platform actions.
8. Failure modes: Premature automated acceptance, hallucinated proof, false opportunity validation, biased matching, automated pressure, and unreviewed publication.
9. Recovery paths: Default deny, human review, rollback, quarantine, correction, kill switch, and policy version restore.
10. Strategic implication: Automation should scale proven judgment, not invent the judgment standard.

## MAJOR POINT D: CAPACITY AND GROWTH CONTROL

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: Limit the cohort and invitation rate.
2. Root cause: Trust declines when intake grows faster than useful response capacity.
3. First order consequence: Footprint pauses invitations before cases become stale.
4. Second order consequence: Existing participants receive consistent care instead of being displaced by growth.
5. Third order consequence: Network reputation compounds around responsiveness and restraint.
6. Upstream dependencies: Case volume, steward capacity, response age, route completion, incident state, and demand balance.
7. Downstream dependencies: Invitation budget, public calls to action, wait language, cohort expansion, and staffing needs.
8. Failure modes: Hidden backlog, performative waitlists, builder heavy imbalance, talent partner spam, and growth targets overriding service quality.
9. Recovery paths: Pause intake, close stale demand, add trained capacity, reduce public promotion, and resume only after service metrics recover.
10. Strategic implication: Controlled scarcity protects service quality only when criteria and wait states remain honest and fair.

## CONSTRUCTIVE CRITICISM

The strongest strategic shift is treating private service as the network building mechanism. This gives Footprint a real reason to exist before it has reach.

The highest leverage improvement is a concrete case standard that makes each judgment explainable, reviewable, and correctable.

The largest over engineering risk is building a complex autonomous control plane before the founding team proves the human service loop on real cases.

The largest under engineering risk is running the network through informal messages and personal memory without durable consent, evidence, decisions, deadlines, and receipts.

## DESTRUCTIVE CRITICISM

### FAILURE PATH 1: CURATED CLUB

Trigger: Invitations favor friends, founders, visible personalities, or socially comfortable people.

Probability: Medium.

Impact: Catastrophic for fairness and external trust.

Mitigation: Record relevance reasons, use evidence and demand criteria, review route distribution, and apply founder neutral gates.

Residual risk: Medium because human relationships still influence discovery.

### FAILURE PATH 2: HIGH TOUCH BOTTLENECK

Trigger: Personal service produces strong quality but cannot respond as the network grows.

Probability: High.

Impact: High.

Mitigation: Capacity limits, invitation pauses, trained role combinations, templated procedures, and automation of deterministic work.

Residual risk: Medium until real case timing is measured.

### FAILURE PATH 3: QUALITY THEATER

Trigger: Forms, scores, and receipts create the appearance of rigor while reviewers approve weak evidence or irrelevant matches.

Probability: Medium.

Impact: High.

Mitigation: Frozen adversarial cases, independent review, outcome feedback, correction analysis, and claim ceiling enforcement.

Residual risk: Medium because judgment cannot be reduced to checklists alone.

### FAILURE PATH 4: PREMATURE AUTOMATION

Trigger: Agents accept members, validate claims, approve opportunities, or make introductions before their judgment is proven.

Probability: High under the earlier autonomous engine framing.

Impact: Catastrophic at public scale.

Mitigation: Human approval boundary, default deny, frozen evaluation cases, shadow comparisons, rollback, and kill switch.

Residual risk: Low while commissioning gates remain enforced.

### FAILURE PATH 5: SERVICE WITHOUT NETWORK EFFECT

Trigger: Footprint helps people privately but does not create cross side understanding, introductions, referrals, or outcomes.

Probability: Medium.

Impact: High.

Mitigation: Measure useful routes, mutual introductions, repeat participation, outcomes, and earned referrals rather than satisfaction alone.

Residual risk: Medium until the founding cohort proves mutual value.

### FAILURE PATH 6: PUBLIC CONTENT TAKES OVER

Trigger: The team spends more time producing posts than reviewing proof, clarifying demand, responding to people, and following outcomes.

Probability: High because public activity is visible and easy to count.

Impact: High.

Mitigation: Service before publication, maximum content allocation, case response service level, and safe silence.

Residual risk: Low when operating metrics govern capacity.
