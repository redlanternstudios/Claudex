## OBJECTIVE

Define a rich Footprint ecosystem that serves tech builders and talent partners without creating duplicate communities, crowded participation, feature sprawl, confusing communication, or hidden founder promotion.

## PROMPT CONTRACT

### GOAL

Create one coherent professional network that produces useful outcomes between tech builders and talent partners while keeping the first version small enough to understand, govern, and operate safely.

The first ninety day success condition is:

1. Twenty verified tech builders.
2. Ten active talent partners.
3. Ten completed useful routes.
4. Five qualified mutual introductions.
5. Three interviews, paid projects, hiring pipelines, or equivalent verified opportunity outcomes.
6. Zero consent, identity, privacy, or platform incidents.

### CONSTRAINTS

1. Footprint remains one public brand and one network.
2. The public network has two sides: tech builders and talent partners.
3. Recruiters, head hunters, and staffing agencies are talent partner subtypes, not separate product worlds.
4. Communication, responses, outreach, and posts must branch by network side and current intent.
5. Homira may operate the public founding account and invite relevant people through permitted LinkedIn actions.
6. Ro and Keymon remain behind the scenes during initial trust formation.
7. By Red, LLC owns Footprint. RedLantern Studios builds and operates the system under By Red authority.
8. Footprint may not promise employment, placement, virality, reach, or introductions.
9. Consent, proof, fairness, relevance, privacy, and sanctioned platform access govern every public action.
10. n8n owns business logic. Supabase owns durable state. SwarmClaw performs bounded evaluation and execution.
11. The current Footprint implementation HOLD remains until the existing preflight blockers are resolved.
12. Footprint is free for builders, talent partners, employers, agencies, and every other participant. Footprint does not charge participation, subscription, listing, introduction, placement, success, or outcome fees.

### FORMAT

This CTP defines the participant model, communication split, minimum surfaces, operating loops, density controls, expansion gates, acceptance criteria, and final recommendation.

### FAILURE

This system fails if it creates separate disconnected communities for each audience, sends the same message to both sides, adds a social feed or marketplace before density exists, treats reaction volume as trust, overwhelms talent partners with weak profiles, overwhelms builders with generic job content, or allows founder promotion to become the hidden purpose.

## PROBLEM STATEMENT

Type: PRODUCT, BEHAVIORAL, OPERATIONS, AND STRATEGIC.

VERIFIED: Tech builders and talent partners participate for different reasons, use different language, require different evidence, and respond to different calls to action.

VERIFIED: Treating every participant the same would produce generic content and low quality matching.

ASSUMED: Creating separate brands, pages, feeds, or communities for recruiters, head hunters, and staffing agencies would fragment early network density and multiply operating complexity before the value loop is proven.

The problem is to preserve meaningful specialization while keeping one understandable ecosystem.

## THREE PASS ANALYSIS

### PASS 1: SURFACE

Footprint appears to need a community for tech builders and another community for recruiters, head hunters, and staffing agencies.

### PASS 2: CHALLENGE

Separate communities would divide scarce early activity, duplicate profiles and content, complicate consent and analytics, and make Footprint feel like several unfinished products.

Recruiters, head hunters, and staffing agencies share one core job inside Footprint: finding credible capability for a real demand. Their commercial structures differ, but they do not need separate ecosystems.

### PASS 3: REAL TRUTH

Footprint needs one network with two intent systems.

Tech builders supply verified capability and seek recognition, feedback, relationships, projects, or roles.

Talent partners supply demand, market context, access, and opportunity pathways.

Footprint creates value by translating between those sides without collapsing their communication into generic language.

Pass 3 governs this CTP.

## MAJOR POINT A: PARTICIPANT STRUCTURE

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: Four named audiences appear to require four member types.
2. Root cause: Recruiters, head hunters, and staffing agencies have different business labels but share a demand side function.
3. First order consequence: Consolidating them as talent partners reduces duplicate flows.
4. Second order consequence: Shared demand data makes builder proof easier to compare with real opportunity needs.
5. Third order consequence: Footprint can learn which evidence produces relevant conversations across multiple talent channels.
6. Upstream dependencies: Role declaration, subtype, organization, intent, authority, consent, and current need.
7. Downstream dependencies: Intake, content selection, response routing, introductions, analytics, and outcome receipts.
8. Failure modes: Wrong role classification, mixed personal and agency interests, fake opportunities, duplicate organizations, and recruiter spam.
9. Recovery paths: Clarification, role correction, organization verification, opportunity withdrawal, restriction, and audit history.
10. Strategic implication: One shared participant model preserves density while still supporting meaningful specialization.

## MAJOR POINT B: BIFURCATED COMMUNICATION

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: Both sides receive Footprint messages.
2. Root cause: Builders want their capability understood, while talent partners want credible capability connected to real demand.
3. First order consequence: One generic message under serves both sides.
4. Second order consequence: Low relevance causes silence, unfollows, spam reports, and weak network learning.
5. Third order consequence: Footprint becomes known as automated noise instead of trusted professional translation.
6. Upstream dependencies: Role, subtype, intent, conversation history, consent, evidence, current demand, and saturation state.
7. Downstream dependencies: Response copy, outreach, post selection, calls to action, landing paths, matching, and measurement.
8. Failure modes: Wrong audience branch, repeated copy, false personalization, promises, pressure, and exposure of private need.
9. Recovery paths: Stop the action, correct the role or intent, apologize when needed, preserve the receipt, and require review before resuming that branch.
10. Strategic implication: Audience specific communication is part of the product, not merely marketing copy.

### BUILDER COMMUNICATION CONTRACT

Purpose: Help a tech builder make real capability understandable and move toward a useful professional outcome.

Voice: Curious, specific, generous, honest, and encouraging without hype.

Footprint asks:

1. What did you build?
2. What real problem did it address?
3. What changed because of your work?
4. What proof can be shared?
5. What kind of opportunity or feedback would be useful now?

Builder responses may route to:

1. Clarification.
2. Proof improvement.
3. Positioning guidance.
4. Community spotlight.
5. Build receipt.
6. Relevant resource.
7. Qualified introduction.
8. Safe silence when no useful route is ready.

Builder calls to action:

1. Share what you are building.
2. Show the result or lesson.
3. Request feedback.
4. State the opportunity you are ready for.

Builder communication may never promise placement, employment, reach, or selection.

### TALENT PARTNER COMMUNICATION CONTRACT

Purpose: Help a recruiter, head hunter, or staffing agency find credible builders and express real demand clearly.

Voice: Concise, evidence led, practical, respectful, and direct.

Footprint asks:

1. What outcome does the role, client, or team need?
2. Which capabilities are essential?
3. What evidence would make a builder credible?
4. What constraints affect location, availability, compensation, engagement type, or timing?
5. What information may Footprint share and with whom?

Talent partner responses may route to:

1. Demand clarification.
2. Opportunity normalization.
3. Builder proof discovery.
4. Capability brief.
5. Qualified introduction request.
6. Market insight.
7. Opportunity correction or withdrawal.
8. Safe silence when the demand is weak, unsafe, discriminatory, or unverifiable.

Talent partner calls to action:

1. Share the outcome you need.
2. Describe the proof that matters.
3. Request a qualified introduction.
4. Contribute a useful market signal.

Talent partner communication may never expose a confidential search, claim that Footprint endorses an employer, or convert a private need into public content without permission.

### BRIDGE COMMUNICATION CONTRACT

Purpose: Help both sides understand each other without forcing either side to adopt the language of the other.

Bridge content includes:

1. What hiring teams actually need to see in builder proof.
2. What builders need from a credible opportunity description.
3. How to distinguish demonstrated capability from polished presentation.
4. How to prepare for an introduction without promising an outcome.
5. Lessons from completed matches with consent and private details removed.

Every bridge post names one primary audience and one secondary benefit. No post attempts to speak equally to everyone.

### Builder intents

1. Share work.
2. Request feedback.

### Talent partner intents

1. Share an opportunity.
2. Request talent discovery.
3. Request an introduction.
4. Share market insight.
5. Verify a builder claim.
6. Correct or withdraw information.

### Unknown intent

## OUTREACH ROUTING

Homira may use permitted LinkedIn invitations and public participation as the founding administrator.

Builder outreach leads with one specific observation about real work and one useful reason to engage.

Talent partner outreach leads with the quality of Footprint proof and one relevant reason the network may help a current demand.

Footprint does not use generic mass outreach, automated personal connection requests, unsolicited Page messages, copied praise, urgency, or engagement bait.

Every outreach template has a builder version, a talent partner version, and a no send rule.

4. Open Opportunity.

1. BUILDER.

This allocation is a maximum planning guide, not a publishing quota. Safe silence remains valid.

## MAJOR POINT C: MINIMUM SURFACE MODEL

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: A full network appears to need a Page, Group, newsletter, site, profile system, opportunity board, feed, chat, and control room.
2. Root cause: Product planning often confuses possible surfaces with required value.
3. First order consequence: Too many early surfaces create empty states and divided attention.
4. Second order consequence: Empty surfaces weaken trust and multiply moderation work.
5. Third order consequence: The team spends time maintaining containers instead of producing useful outcomes.
6. Upstream dependencies: Real intake, proof, demand, consent, response capacity, and repeat participation.
7. Downstream dependencies: Public discovery, durable records, matching, operations, analytics, and recovery.
8. Failure modes: Empty Group, stale newsletter, duplicate profiles, unused chat, abandoned opportunity listings, and inconsistent state.
9. Recovery paths: Defer the surface, redirect to the canonical path, archive safely, and preserve records.
10. Strategic implication: Surface restraint protects density and makes later expansion evidence driven.

### VERSION ONE SURFACES

Version one has only three surfaces.

1. LinkedIn Page for discovery, public proof, permitted invitations, messages, comments, and posts.
2. Owned Footprint site for intake, consent, durable builder proof, opportunity details, correction, removal, and outcome records.
3. Private control room for decisions, safety, fairness, schedule pressure, incidents, and the kill switch.

### DEFERRED SURFACES

1. LinkedIn Group waits until repeat participation and moderation capacity pass the density gate.
2. Newsletter waits until Footprint has eight weeks of recurring useful material and a reliable reader promise.
3. Native chat is not built in version one. LinkedIn and approved contact paths carry conversation.
4. A native social feed is not built. LinkedIn is the public feed.
5. Separate builder and recruiter portals are not built. One site provides role aware paths.
6. Rankings, badges, streaks, popularity scores, follower systems, and public leaderboards are prohibited.

## CORE OPERATING LOOPS

### Builder loop

Observe work, invite or receive, clarify, verify proof, provide a useful route, request consent when public use is proposed, publish or introduce when eligible, measure the outcome.

### Talent partner loop

Observe demand, invite or receive, clarify the needed outcome, verify the organization and opportunity, identify relevant proof, request mutual consent, introduce, measure the outcome.

### Bridge loop

Observe recurring mismatch, translate it into useful guidance, publish for one primary audience, measure whether behavior becomes clearer, update the guidance.

### Trust loop

Record consent, claims, decisions, corrections, exposure, and outcomes. Use the record to improve relevance without weakening hard safety rules.

## MAJOR POINT D: DENSITY AND SATURATION GOVERNOR

### TEN LAYERS

1. Surface: More participants and more content appear to create more value.
2. Root cause: A network creates value only when response capacity, relevance, and mutual demand remain healthy.
3. First order consequence: Uncontrolled growth increases unanswered submissions and weak matches.
4. Second order consequence: Weak service teaches both sides that Footprint is noisy and unreliable.
5. Third order consequence: High volume produces permanent reputation damage and distorted learning.
6. Upstream dependencies: Queue depth, response time, role balance, demand quality, proof quality, consent, moderation capacity, and incident state.
7. Downstream dependencies: Invitations, publication, introductions, cohort expansion, and new surfaces.
8. Failure modes: Builder oversupply, talent partner spam, repeated identities, duplicate opportunities, broad low quality outreach, and slow correction.
9. Recovery paths: Pause intake, narrow invitations, reduce publishing, close stale opportunities, prioritize existing members, and require a capacity receipt before reopening.
10. Strategic implication: Controlled density produces more trust than raw audience size.

### FOUNDING COHORT

1. Maximum twenty tech builders.
2. Maximum ten active talent partners.
3. Talent partner subtypes share the same ten person capacity pool.
4. No new cohort opens while more than ten percent of legitimate submissions lack a useful route after five business days.
5. No new cohort opens during an unresolved consent, identity, privacy, or platform incident.

### EXPOSURE LIMITS

1. No individual receives more than ten percent of public features in a rolling thirty day window.
2. No organization receives more than fifteen percent of public features in a rolling thirty day window.
3. Similar topics, formats, and calls to action are compared with recent history before publication.
4. A talent partner may not repeatedly post substantially similar opportunities.
5. A builder may have multiple Proof Records, but Footprint must not convert every update into public content.
6. Founder and studio exposure remains zero during trust formation.

### CAPACITY RULE

Growth follows useful response capacity, not follower count.

When response capacity falls below policy, Footprint pauses invitations and public intake promotion before service quality falls.

## BEHAVIORAL DRIVER SEPARATION

### Tech builders

Behavioral driver: Recognition of real capability, professional identity, progress, useful feedback, and access to opportunity.

Mechanism: Proof Records, spotlights, guidance, introductions, and outcome receipts.

Real constraint: The work must be understandable and credible without forcing the builder to become a content creator.

### Talent partners

Behavioral driver: Reduced search risk, higher confidence, faster understanding, and access to credible capability.

Mechanism: Structured demand, verified proof, capability briefs, and qualified introductions.

Real constraint: Footprint must save attention rather than create another unfiltered candidate pool.

### Footprint

Behavioral driver: Trust earned through repeated usefulness and fair judgment.

Mechanism: Segmented communication, consent, proof, routing, saturation control, and receipts.

Real constraint: One weak or self serving pattern can damage both sides at once.

## CONSTRUCTIVE CRITICISM

The strongest decision is the communication bifurcation. It acknowledges that network value does not come from putting builders and hiring professionals into the same room. It comes from translating proof and demand well enough that each side saves time and gains confidence.

The highest leverage improvement is disciplined role and intent capture at the first interaction. If that classification is wrong, every later message can be polished and still be irrelevant.

The largest over engineering risk is building a native marketplace, feed, chat system, Group, newsletter, and separate portals before Footprint proves qualified introductions.

The largest under engineering risk is treating segmentation as copy templates without durable role, intent, consent, evidence, and outcome state.

## DESTRUCTIVE CRITICISM

### FAILURE PATH 1: BUILDER DIRECTORY

Trigger: Footprint accumulates profiles without producing useful routes or conversations.

Probability: High without demand capture.

Impact: High. Builders feel extracted from and talent partners see another stale database.

Mitigation: Cap cohorts, require useful routes, measure introductions, and pause intake when demand is weak.

Residual risk: Medium.

### FAILURE PATH 2: RECRUITER SPAM CHANNEL

Trigger: Talent partners post broad or repeated opportunities and contact builders without clear consent.

Probability: Medium.

Impact: Catastrophic for builder trust.

Mitigation: Verify organizations and demand, require mutual introduction consent, enforce frequency limits, and restrict repeat offenders.

Residual risk: Low after enforcement.

### FAILURE PATH 3: TWO DISCONNECTED SILOS

Trigger: Builder content and talent partner content never translate into shared understanding or introductions.

Probability: Medium.

Impact: High. Footprint becomes two content calendars with no network effect.

Mitigation: Reserve bridge content, measure mutual outcomes, and require each campaign to name the other side benefit.

Residual risk: Medium until real matches occur.

### FAILURE PATH 4: OVERPOPULATED INTAKE

Trigger: Invitation and submission growth exceeds the ability to respond usefully.

Probability: High if follower growth becomes the goal.

Impact: High. Unanswered people interpret Footprint as careless or deceptive.

Mitigation: Founding cohort caps, response capacity gate, invitation pause, and safe wait language.

Residual risk: Low when pause controls are automatic.

### FAILURE PATH 5: FEATURE SPRAWL

Trigger: Every professional interaction becomes a new surface or product feature.

Probability: High without the minimum surface rule.

Impact: High. Empty states, duplicated data, maintenance cost, and confusing navigation compound.

Mitigation: Three version one surfaces, seven core objects, explicit deferred list, and evidence gates for expansion.

Residual risk: Low while scope governance holds.

### FAILURE PATH 6: HIDDEN FOUNDER FUNNEL

Trigger: Community trust is used to promote Ro, Keymon, RedLantern, or products before the same eligibility rules are satisfied.

Probability: Medium because founder opportunity is an intended later benefit.

Impact: Catastrophic for perceived independence.

Mitigation: Zero founder allocation during trust formation, transparent ownership, equal evidence criteria, exposure ledger, and independent approval.

Residual risk: Medium because audience interpretation cannot be fully controlled.
